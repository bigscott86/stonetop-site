# NPCs & the People of Stonetop

A reference drawn from **Book I — Stonetop**, pages **1–165** (the "Welcome to Stonetop" and "Getting Started" chapters, plus the steading playbook's NPC reference pages). Accuracy and terminology are taken straight from the source; page numbers are cited inline.

What follows is everything NPC-relevant that actually appears in pages 1–165: the setting framing, the in-play procedure for introducing townsfolk, the record formats, and the name/trait lists. ## The village of Stonetop & its people

Stonetop is a "hearth fantasy" village in an "iron age that never was." The PCs are **the heroes of the village of Stonetop**, with deep roots there; the game is about defending it and seizing opportunities for it, not wandering for treasure (p.7–9).

Key facts about the village and its folk (p.10–11, "The setting"):

- **~300 folk.** "A nice place, though poor. People look out for each other." (p.10)
- Built around a **massive standing stone** of unknown origin, carved with faint runes; lightning strikes it often (p.7, 10).
- Stands at the **edge of the Great Wood**. Villagers hunt and trap but — per a compact with the **Forest Folk** — never fell a living tree (p.10).
- Lies in the **World's End**, a sparsely populated region dotted with ruins; the nearest settlement is days away (p.10).
- Built atop **Maker-ruins** (an ancient cistern, the crumbling Old Wall, old foundations). The **Ruined Tower** is about a day from town (p.10).
- All PCs are **human**. Strange peoples exist but are poorly understood: the **Forest Folk**, the **Fae**, the mask-wearing **Ustrina** (p.10).
- The **Forest Folk disappeared ten years ago** and no one knows why — an open mystery for play. **Crinwin** (horrid little creatures) lurk in the Great Wood and have grown bolder since the Forest Folk left (p.10–11).

### Named NPCs / characters introduced in range

The in-range chapters deliberately keep the cast open — players invent and name most townsfolk during character creation (see next section), so **no roster of pre-named villagers** is given here. The named figures that *do* appear in pages 1–165 are:

- **The Judges of other steadings** — referenced in the Judge playbook's introduction questions as *Devin*, *Haeris*, *etc.*, who may be the PC's mentor, a fellow apprentice, or an ambitious/inflexible peer (p.24).
- **Deities invoked by playbooks** (NPC-adjacent powers the PCs are tied to): **Danu** (the Blessed/Vessel), **Helior** (the Lightbearer), **Aratis** (the Judge/Prophet) (p.23–24).
- **The Pale Hunter** — a figure of village legend; a townsfolk trait is "swears they met the Pale Hunter" (p.162).

The PCs themselves — chosen from the playbooks below — are the central "heroes of an isolated village," each a local hero with community ties (p.12):

| Playbook | Role in the village |
|---|---|
| **The Blessed** | Nature priest; speaks to spirits and beasts, works subtle magic |
| **The Fox** | Clever, quick, skillful; bends rules, a charmer |
| **The Heavy** | The village's *own* violent champion — and a liability |
| **The Judge** | Settles disputes, keeps the chronicle, a bulwark against chaos |
| **The Lightbearer** | Invokes divine power via flame; a beacon of hope and mercy |
| **The Marshal** | Leads the militia and a crew of **followers** |
| **The Ranger** | At home in the wild; a guide and deadly hunter |
| **The Seeker** | Collects lost lore and artifacts that may lead to ruin |
| **The Would-be Hero** | Full of fear and anger, in over their head |

## How NPCs work in early play (pp.1–165)

In range, NPCs are handled mostly through **first-session character creation and introductions**, not a formal stat system.

**Adding an NPC (p.22–23, "Intro the PCs").** During introductions, players take turns presenting their characters and answering each other's and the GM's questions. The rule for new townsfolk:

> "Whenever someone wants or needs to add a new NPC to the mix, suggest that they pick one of the unused **names** from the front of their playbook. Additional names are available in the steading playbook. Also give each NPC an **occupation** and/or **trait**." (p.22, pointing to the trait list on p.163)

> "Take notes! Add NPCs to the steading playbook as you go… and update the **Residents of Stonetop** and **Notable Neighbors** [pages] later." (p.22)

So the early-play loop is: a relationship or person comes up in conversation → name them from the lists → assign an occupation and at least one trait → record them on the steading playbook.

**How the village matters.** Adventures are framed as crises that threaten the village or opportunities to improve its lot, punctuated by quiet hearth moments — "Talking to your pa by the hearth. Courting a sweetheart. Bringing in the harvest with your neighbors" (p.7). The premise (p.8) is explicit: PCs "have roots in the village and care about its inhabitants." NPCs are "our kith and our kin" (p.7).

**Playbook-seeded relationships (p.23–24).** The introduction questions in each playbook prompt PCs to invent connected NPCs and ties, e.g.:

- **Mentors** — the Vessel (Blessed) and others are asked "Do you have a mentor?"; the Judge names the prior Judge who trained them and Judges of other steadings (p.23–24).
- **Followers / crew** — the Marshal answers "What is the nature of your crew?" and how they recruit members (p.24). (Crew/followers are the bridge to the later Followers rules at p.449+.)
- **Disciples & rivals** — the Lightbearer names "your most devoted disciple" and "who in town resents you" (p.24).
- **Origin ties** — non-Stonetop PCs answer who came with them and what ties them to the village (p.22).
- **Secrets & reputation** — the Fox is asked "What secret do you know? About whom?" and "Who here knows about your past?" (p.22).

> Note: even trivial NPC details are player-tunable — the content-guidelines example literally lists '"NPCs named *Sean*"' as something a player could ask to exclude (p.18).

## Notable neighbors & settlements

From the setting overview (p.10–11) and the **"Notable neighbors of Stonetop"** reference page (p.164–165):

| Place | Direction / distance | Vibe & notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Gordin's Delve** | ~4 days west, along the West Road | The closest settlement; a **mining town**. "Everyone comes to Gordin's Delve from somewhere else" (so it has no name list of its own). The Hillfolk hold its miners in contempt. (p.10–11, 164) |
| **Marshedge** | ~10 days' march south & east | A **sizeable town**. Some villagers have "a beef with Marshedge." (p.10, 164) |
| **The Steplands / Hillfolk** | Rugged country to the south | The **Hillfolk** are nomads who roam the Steplands and the Flats; they hold Gordin's Delve in contempt. A townsfolk trait is "hates the Hillfolk." (p.10–11, 164) |
| **Barrier Pass** | A few days north, in the mountains | "Stoic, grim, unwelcoming." Listed under "Other places." (p.10, 164) |
| **Lygos (and points south)** | Far to the south | The **closest city** of the more "civilized" south. (p.10–11, 164) |
| **The Manmarch / other places** | South | Grouped with Barrier Pass under "Other places." (p.164) |
| **The Flats** | West | Open country roamed by the Hillfolk; where the first wildflowers bloom. (p.7, 10) |
| **The Great Wood** | East/adjacent | Forest the village borders; home of the vanished Forest Folk, the Fae, and crinwin. (p.10) |

There is **no empire, kings, or nobles** locally; more "civilized" towns and cities lie farther south, of which **Lygos** is the closest (p.10).

## The NPC record format

Two parallel record formats appear on the steading playbook, both built on the same skeleton:

**Residents of Stonetop (p.163):**
> "Add each NPC that is named during introductions or play. Give each an occupation (even if just farmer or homemaker) and **at least 1 trait**."
>
> Columns: **name (pronouns)** · **occupation** · **traits, relations, etc.**
> Pre-listed occupations to fill: *publican, smith, tanner, potter, midwife*.

**Notable neighbors of Stonetop (p.164):**
> "Add NPCs from neighboring areas when instructed by a playbook, or as needed."
>
> Columns: **name (pronouns)** · **home** · **occupation** · **traits, relations, etc.**

### Template (one row per NPC)

```
name (pronouns) | home | occupation | traits, relations, etc.
```

- **name** — pick from the regional lists above (or invent).
- **pronouns** — recorded alongside the name.
- **home** — Stonetop, or a neighboring place (used on the Neighbors record).
- **occupation** — required, even if just "farmer" or "homemaker." Examples: publican, smith, tanner, potter, midwife.
- **traits, relations** — at least one trait; relationships to other NPCs/PCs noted free-form here.

## → Website note: mapping to the NPC relationship node graph

The book's own data model maps almost one-to-one onto the planned **node graph** feature (CLAUDE.md "Roadmap → 1. NPC relationship node graph") and the existing **Common House → "Townsfolk & Relationships"** section (`Mesh_Longhouse`, an editable building).

**Nodes = NPCs.** Each node should carry the book's record fields:

```jsonc
{
  "id": "...",
  "name": "Mered",
  "pronouns": "she/her",
  "home": "Stonetop",          // or Marshedge, the Steplands, Lygos, …
  "occupation": "midwife",      // required, per p.163
  "traits": ["knows all the gossip", "loyal friend"],  // ≥1, from p.163 list
  "x": 0, "y": 0, "note": ""
}
```

This extends the current flat `npcs:[{name, role}]` schema (CLAUDE.md) — `role` becomes `occupation`, and `pronouns` / `home` / `traits` are added. The roadmap's proposed shape `{ nodes:[{id,name,x,y,note}], edges:[{from,to,label}] }` should absorb these extra fields on each node.

**Edges = relations.** The "traits, relations, etc." column is exactly what the **edges** capture. Suggested labeled edge types (drawn from the in-range material): `family`, `married`, `sweetheart`, `mentor`, `apprentice`, `crew/follower`, `rival`, `resents`, `disciple`, `ally`, `friend`. The roadmap already lists "ally," "rival," "family" as examples — extend with the relationship-flavored traits above.

**Generator hook.** The regional name lists are ready-made seed data for a "random townsfolk" button: pick a name from the chosen region's list, assign an occupation (publican / smith / tanner / potter / midwife / farmer / homemaker…), and roll 1+ traits from the p.163 list — exactly the p.22 procedure, automated.

**Home as a grouping/filter.** The Neighbors record's `home` column maps to a node attribute you can filter or color by region (Stonetop, Marshedge, Steplands, Lygos, Gordin's Delve, Barrier Pass).
